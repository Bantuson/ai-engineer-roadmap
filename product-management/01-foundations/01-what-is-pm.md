# What is Product Management?

## Learning Objectives

- [ ] Define what a product manager does and doesn't do
- [ ] Distinguish PM from project management, engineering, and design roles
- [ ] Identify the different types of PM roles (B2C, B2B, Platform, Technical, AI)
- [ ] Understand the core PM skill set

## Prerequisites

None—this is your starting point.

---

## Core Content

### The Product Manager Role

A product manager is responsible for the **success of a product**. That sounds simple, but unpacking it reveals the complexity:

**Success** means different things at different stages:
- For a startup: finding product-market fit
- For a growth product: increasing adoption and engagement
- For a mature product: optimizing efficiency and defending market share

**Responsible** doesn't mean doing everything yourself. PMs:
- **Don't** write the code
- **Don't** create the designs
- **Don't** run the marketing campaigns
- **Don't** manage people (usually)

**Do** ensure all those pieces come together to create something users want.

### The "CEO of the Product" Myth

You'll hear this phrase a lot. It's misleading.

**What it gets right:**
- PMs think holistically about the product
- PMs balance business needs, user needs, and technical constraints
- PMs own the product strategy

**What it gets wrong:**
- PMs don't have CEO authority—they can't hire/fire
- PMs influence through persuasion, not mandate
- PMs are collaborators, not dictators

**A better metaphor:** PMs are like orchestra conductors. They don't play every instrument, but they ensure all the musicians play together beautifully.

### PM vs. Project Manager

This confusion persists despite being different roles:

| Aspect | Product Manager | Project Manager |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Focus** | What to build and why | How to build it on time |
| **Timeframe** | Product lifetime | Project duration |
| **Success metric** | User/business outcomes | On-time, on-budget delivery |
| **Key question** | "Should we build this?" | "How do we deliver this?" |
| **Stakeholders** | Users, business, engineering | Team, stakeholders, sponsors |

**Overlap exists:** In smaller companies, one person may do both. In larger organizations, they're distinct roles that collaborate closely.

### PM vs. Other Roles

**PM vs. Engineering Manager:**
- PM: What to build, priorities, requirements
- EM: How to build, team health, technical decisions

**PM vs. Designer:**
- PM: User problems to solve, success metrics, constraints
- Designer: User experience, interface, interaction patterns

**PM vs. Marketing:**
- PM: Product capabilities, positioning foundation
- Marketing: Go-to-market, messaging, demand generation

**The collaboration reality:** Great products emerge from PM, design, and engineering working as a triad, not from PMs dictating to others.

### Types of Product Managers

The PM role varies significantly based on context:

#### Consumer PM (B2C)
**What they do:** Build products for individual users
**Key skills:** User psychology, viral growth, engagement metrics
**Examples:** Instagram features, Spotify playlists, mobile games
**Mindset:** "How do millions of people experience this?"

#### Enterprise/B2B PM
**What they do:** Build products for business customers
**Key skills:** Sales enablement, procurement cycles, ROI articulation
**Examples:** Salesforce features, Slack enterprise, AWS services
**Mindset:** "How does this create business value?"

#### Platform PM
**What they do:** Build products that other products build on
**Key skills:** API design, developer experience, ecosystem thinking
**Examples:** Stripe APIs, Twilio, AWS Lambda
**Mindset:** "How do developers build with this?"

#### Technical PM
**What they do:** Work on technically complex products, often infrastructure
**Key skills:** Deep technical understanding, engineering collaboration
**Examples:** Database systems, ML infrastructure, performance tools
**Mindset:** "How do we solve hard technical problems?"

#### AI/ML PM
**What they do:** Manage products with AI/ML components
**Key skills:** ML fundamentals, evaluation methods, responsible AI
**Examples:** Recommendation systems, ChatGPT features, AI assistants
**Mindset:** "How do we ship non-deterministic systems?"

#### Growth PM
**What they do:** Focus on user acquisition, activation, retention
**Key skills:** Experimentation, funnel analysis, behavioral psychology
**Examples:** Onboarding flows, referral programs, notification systems
**Mindset:** "How do we move the metrics?"

### The Core PM Skill Set

Regardless of PM type, certain skills are universal:

#### 1. Problem Definition
The ability to clearly articulate:
- Who has this problem?
- How painful is it?
- What does success look like?

**Why it matters:** Building the right thing trumps building the thing right.

#### 2. Prioritization
Deciding what to do—and crucially, what NOT to do—given limited resources.

**The hard truth:** You'll always have more ideas than capacity. PMs who try to do everything fail.

#### 3. Communication
Translating between stakeholders who speak different languages:
- Technical concepts for business stakeholders
- Business constraints for engineers
- User needs for everyone

**Medium matters:** Written PRDs, verbal presentations, roadmap visuals—PMs must master multiple formats.

#### 4. User Empathy
Understanding users beyond surface-level requests:
- What they say they want
- What they actually need
- What they don't know they want yet

**The PM paradox:** Users often can't articulate their problems, but PMs must solve them anyway.

#### 5. Data Literacy
Using data to inform decisions:
- Quantitative: metrics, A/B tests, funnels
- Qualitative: user research, feedback synthesis
- Knowing when data isn't enough

**The trap:** Data can prove anything. PMs must avoid confirmation bias.

#### 6. Technical Fluency
Understanding enough about how things work to:
- Have meaningful conversations with engineers
- Make informed tradeoff decisions
- Spot when something is harder or easier than it seems

**Note:** This doesn't mean you need to code, but you need to understand the building blocks.

### What Great PMs Do Differently

**They say no more than yes.**
Protecting the team from scope creep and distraction is as valuable as identifying opportunities.

**They write things down.**
Documentation creates alignment, reduces miscommunication, and scales your influence.

**They stay curious.**
Great PMs ask "why" relentlessly—about user behavior, competitive moves, technical constraints.

**They own outcomes, not outputs.**
Shipping features isn't success. Achieving user/business outcomes is success.

**They adapt their approach.**
What works at a startup fails at an enterprise. What works in B2C fails in B2B. Context matters.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **PMs are responsible for product success, achieved through influence rather than authority**
2. **The role varies significantly across B2C, B2B, Platform, Technical, AI, and Growth contexts**
3. **Core skills include problem definition, prioritization, communication, user empathy, data literacy, and technical fluency**
4. **Great PMs focus on outcomes over outputs and say no more than yes**
5. **PM is not project management—the distinction matters**
